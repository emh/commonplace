Quote · entered Apr 22 · from Kindle
The frontiers of a book are never clear-cut: beyond the title, the first lines, and the last full stop, beyond its internal configuration and its autonomous form, it is caught up in a system of references to other books, other texts, other sentences: it is a node within a network.
Michel Foucault, The Archaeology of Knowledge
p. 23 · highlighted in Kindle · loc. 412 · re-read twice
Discussion · 4 turns · 2 branches
You asked
What does Foucault mean by “node within a network” here? Is it metaphorical, or is he proposing something structural about how meaning itself gets made?
Response

It's structural, not decorative. Foucault is dismantling the idea of the book as a self-contained object — a work that “speaks for itself.” For him, a book only becomes legible through its relations with other books, other utterances, other institutions. The title page is a polite fiction.

Think of it this way: every sentence in a text silently cites, contradicts, or depends on utterances made elsewhere — by other authors, by earlier versions of the same thinker, by the surrounding culture. A book is less a container than an intersection of discursive currents. Pull at one and the whole network shivers.

Branch → intersection of discursive currents
You asked
How is this different from intertextuality as Kristeva defines it? Foucault seems to be claiming something stronger.
Response

Sharp instinct. Kristeva's intertextuality (1966) is centered on the text as a mosaic of quotations — any text absorbs and transforms others. It's a theory about the text.

Foucault is up one level: he's interested not in texts but in the discursive formations that make certain statements possible, sayable, true-seeming. Kristeva tells you the book is woven. Foucault tells you the loom is historical and political.

You followed
If a book is a node, what's the corresponding unit for reading? Is there an equivalent network on the reader's side?
Response

Yes — and Foucault gestures at it indirectly through his later work on the archive. The reader's counterpart to the book-node is not an individual but a system of what one is prepared to read as meaningful at all. Your training, your earlier reading, the institutions that taught you what counts as a citation versus mere coincidence.

In other words: reading is also a node, not an act. What feels like recognition — “I've seen this before” — is the network noticing itself.
